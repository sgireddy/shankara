# निर्वाण षट्कम् — Nirvana Shatakam — Verse V: Detailed Commentary

## ॐ

---

## The Verse (श्लोकः)

### Sanskrit (संस्कृतम्)

> न मे मृत्युशङ्का न मे जातिभेदः
> पिता नैव मे नैव माता न जन्म ।
> न बन्धुर्न मित्रं गुरुर्नैव शिष्यः
> चिदानन्द रूपः शिवोऽहं शिवोऽहम् ॥

### Telugu (తెలుగు)

> న మే మృత్యుశంకా న మే జాతిభేదః
> పితా నైవ మే నైవ మాతా న జన్మ ।
> న బంధుర్న మిత్రం గురుర్నైవ శిష్యః
> చిదానంద రూపః శివోఽహం శివోఽహమ్ ॥

### Transliteration

> Na me mṛtyuśhaṅkā na me jātibhedaḥ
> Pitā naiva me naiva mātā na janma
> Na bandhur na mitram gurur naiva śhiṣhyaḥ
> Chidānanda rūpaḥ Śhivo'ham Śhivo'ham

### English Translation

> I have no fear of death. I have no caste distinction.
> I have no father, no mother, no birth.
> I have no relative, no friend, no guru, no disciple.
> I am of the nature of Consciousness-Bliss. I am Shiva. I am Shiva.

---

## Theme: Stripping Death and Relations (మృత్యువు మరియు బంధాల నిరాకరణ)

The previous verses stripped away the instruments of experience (body, mind, senses), the content of experience (emotions, desires, morality), and the framework of experience (karma, religion, the experiencer itself). In Verse V, Śaṅkara strikes at the two most primal human anxieties: **death** and **identity through relationships**. This verse addresses the existential core of being human — the fear of annihilation and the web of belonging that gives ordinary life its meaning and structure.

---

## Pāda-by-Pāda (Line-by-Line) Commentary

### Pāda 1: *Na me mṛtyuśhaṅkā na me jātibhedaḥ*

**"I have no fear of death. I have no caste distinction."**

#### Mṛtyu Śaṅkā — The Fear of Death

*Mṛtyu* (death) is the ultimate fear — what Patañjali's Yoga Sūtras (2.9) call *abhiniveśha*:

> *Svarasavāhī viduṣho'pi tathārūḍho'bhiniveśhaḥ*
> "Clinging to life flows on by its own momentum, and is rooted even in the wise."

This primal terror of non-existence persists even in the learned. It is not merely an intellectual fear but a deep, instinctive recoil embedded in every living being. Even animals, who have never contemplated death philosophically, flee from it.

Śaṅkara declares: **the Self has no fear of death** — not because it has conquered fear through courage, but because **death does not apply to the Self**. Death is the departure of consciousness from a particular body. But the Self is not a body-dwelling entity; it is consciousness itself — unbounded, unborn, and undying.

The Bhagavad Gītā (2.20) states:

> *Na jāyate mriyate vā kadāchit / Nāyam bhūtvā bhavitā vā na bhūyaḥ / Ajo nityaḥ śhāśhvato'yam purāṇo / Na hanyate hanyamāne śharīre*
> "The Self is never born, nor does it ever die. Having come to be, it will never cease to be. It is unborn, eternal, ever-existing, primeval. It is not slain when the body is slain."

If the Self was never born, it cannot die. If it cannot die, there is nothing to fear. The fear of death is the ego's fear — the fear of the *apparent* self, not the *real* Self.

#### Jāti Bheda — Caste Distinction

*Jāti* (birth-class / caste) and *bheda* (distinction / division) together refer to the social identity system that was (and remains) deeply significant in Indian society. One's jāti determines one's social role, marriage pool, ritual obligations, and in traditional society, one's very sense of self.

Śaṅkara's negation here is radical in its social implications. He declares: **the Self has no caste**. Caste belongs to the body and to social convention. The Ātman is neither Brāhmaṇa nor Śūdra, neither high nor low. This teaching echoes the Chāndogya Upaniṣad's famous story of Satyakāma Jābāla (4.4), where a boy of uncertain parentage is accepted as a student because of his truthfulness — the implication being that spiritual qualification transcends birth.

Śaṅkara himself, in the *Manīṣā Pañchakam*, composed when a *chāṇḍāla* (untouchable) challenged him on the streets of Kāśī, declared:

> *Chāṇḍālo'stu sa tu dvijo'stu gurur ity eṣhā manīṣhā mama*
> "Whether one is an outcaste or a twice-born, if one has realized the Self, that one is my guru — this is my firm conviction."

The Self-realized being sees only the Ātman everywhere; social distinctions dissolve in that vision.

---

### Pāda 2: *Pitā naiva me naiva mātā na janma*

**"I have no father, no mother, no birth."**

#### Negating Biological Origin

The deepest anchor of personal identity is one's origin: "I was born to these parents, in this family, at this time and place." This creates the foundational narrative of "my life" — the story from which all other stories branch.

Śaṅkara demolishes this narrative entirely:

| Sanskrit | Transliteration | Meaning | What It Negates |
|---|---|---|---|
| पिता (pitā) | Pitā | Father | Paternal lineage, *gotra* (clan) |
| माता (mātā) | Mātā | Mother | Maternal connection, the womb, physical origin |
| जन्म (janma) | Janma | Birth | The event of coming into being; the beginning of the biographical self |

If the Self has no birth (*janma*), then it has no parents, no lineage, no starting point. This is not a denial of biological reality — the body obviously has parents. It is a declaration that the **Self** was never born. As the Kaṭha Upaniṣad (1.2.18) affirms:

> *Na jāyate mriyate vā vipaśchit / Nāyam kutaśchit na babhūva kaśchit / Ajo nityaḥ śhāśhvato'yam purāṇo / Na hanyate hanyamāne śharīre*
> "The knowing Self is not born, nor does it die. It did not spring from anything, nor did anything spring from it. It is unborn, eternal, everlasting, ancient. It is not killed when the body is killed."

#### The Unborn Self

The concept of *aja* (unborn) is one of the most important attributes of Brahman/Ātman in the Upaniṣads. If the Self had a beginning, it would be an effect (*kārya*), and all effects are impermanent. An impermanent Self cannot be the ultimate refuge. Therefore, the Self must be *aja* — beginningless — and consequently without parents or origin of any kind.

---

### Pāda 3: *Na bandhur na mitram gurur naiva śhiṣhyaḥ*

**"I have no relative, no friend, no guru, no disciple."**

#### Negating the Relational Self

This is perhaps the most emotionally challenging line in the entire Nirvana Shatakam. Human beings are relational creatures; our identities are woven from relationships. "I am a son, a father, a friend, a student, a teacher" — these roles constitute the social self.

| Sanskrit | Transliteration | Meaning | Relational Category |
|---|---|---|---|
| बन्धुः (bandhuḥ) | Bandhu | Relative, kinsman | Blood relations, extended family |
| मित्रम् (mitram) | Mitra | Friend | Chosen bonds of affection and loyalty |
| गुरुः (guruḥ) | Guru | Teacher, spiritual master | The sacred teacher-student relationship |
| शिष्यः (śhiṣhyaḥ) | Śhiṣhya | Disciple, student | The recipient of spiritual transmission |

#### The Guru-Śiṣya Negation

The negation of *guru* and *śhiṣhya* is especially striking. In Hindu tradition, the guru-śiṣhya relationship is considered the most sacred of all bonds — the vehicle through which liberating knowledge is transmitted. The Muṇḍaka Upaniṣad (1.2.12) declares:

> *Tad vijñānārtham sa gurum evābhigachchhet / Samit-pāṇiḥ śrotriyam brahma-niṣhṭham*
> "To know That, let him approach a guru — one who is learned in the Vedas and established in Brahman."

How can Śaṅkara — who himself had a guru (Govindapāda) and countless śhiṣhyas — negate this relationship?

The answer lies in the distinction between *vyāvahārika* (empirical) and *pāramārthika* (absolute) levels of truth:

- **Empirically**, the guru-śiṣhya relationship is real and indispensable. Without a guru, the teaching cannot be received.
- **Ultimately**, once the teaching is absorbed and the Self is realized, the distinction between guru and śiṣhya dissolves. There is only one Self — appearing as both teacher and student, just as the same ocean appears as different waves.

Śaṅkara expressed this beautifully in the *Dakṣhiṇāmūrti Stotra*:

> *Viśhvam darpaṇa-dṛśhyamāna-nagarī-tulyam nijāntargatam*
> "The universe is like a city seen in a mirror — it appears within the Self."

In that mirror, who is the guru and who is the disciple?

#### The Bṛhadāraṇyaka Teaching

The Bṛhadāraṇyaka Upaniṣad (4.3.22) describes the state of Self-realization:

> *Tatra pitā apitā bhavati, mātā amātā, lokā alokāḥ, devā adevāḥ, vedā avedāḥ*
> "There [in the state of Self-knowledge], a father is not a father, a mother is not a mother, worlds are not worlds, gods are not gods, Vedas are not Vedas."

All relational identities dissolve — not because relationships cease to exist in the empirical world, but because the Self is recognized as the single, non-dual reality that underlies all apparent relationships.

---

### Pāda 4 (Refrain): *Chidānanda rūpaḥ Śhivo'ham Śhivo'ham*

**"I am of the nature of Consciousness-Bliss. I am Shiva. I am Shiva."**

After the dissolution of death, birth, lineage, and all human bonds, the refrain arrives as the ultimate anchor. Everything that the mind clings to for identity has been removed — and yet, the Self is not diminished, orphaned, or alone. It is *Chidānanda* — Consciousness-Bliss — full, complete, and self-sufficient.

There is a profound emotional arc in this verse: the ego experiences the negations as a kind of death, a terrible aloneness. But the refrain transforms this apparent void into infinite fullness. The Self is not alone because there is no "other" from which to be separated. It is *kevala* — alone in the sense of *all-one*.

## Philosophical Connections

### Abhiniveśha — The Primal Clinging to Life

Patañjali identifies *abhiniveśha* (clinging to life / fear of death) as one of the five *kleśhas* (afflictions) that bind the soul. The five kleśhas are:

1. **Avidyā** — Ignorance (mistaking the non-Self for the Self)
2. **Asmitā** — "I-am-ness" (identifying consciousness with the ego)
3. **Rāga** — Attachment
4. **Dveṣha** — Aversion
5. **Abhiniveśha** — Fear of death / clinging to life

Verse V directly addresses the fifth kleśha. When the Self is known as unborn and undying, abhiniveśha dissolves at its root. This is not a psychological technique for managing fear — it is an ontological recognition that eliminates the very basis of the fear.

### Āśhrama Dharma and Relational Identity

Hindu society is structured around four āśhramas (stages of life): *brahmacharya* (student), *gṛhastha* (householder), *vānaprastha* (forest dweller), and *sannyāsa* (renunciant). Each stage defines the individual through a web of relationships and duties. By negating all relational roles, Śaṅkara points to a state beyond even *sannyāsa* — the *turīyātīta* or *avadhūta* state, where even the identity "I am a renunciant" is abandoned.

The Avadhūta Gītā (1.1) proclaims:

> *Īśhvarānugraheṇaiva puṃsām advaita-vāsanā*
> "By the grace of the Lord alone does the conviction of non-duality arise in beings."

### Social Revolution Within Spiritual Teaching

Śaṅkara's negation of *jātibheda* (caste distinction) carries profound social implications. By declaring the Self beyond caste, he undermines the metaphysical basis for caste discrimination. If the Ātman has no jāti, then treating someone as inferior or superior based on birth is a category error — mistaking the body's social label for the Self's nature.

This teaching was lived out by many Advaitic saints who followed Śaṅkara, including Śrī Rāmakṛṣhṇa, Svāmī Vivekānanda, and Śrī Ramaṇa Maharṣhi, all of whom explicitly taught the irrelevance of caste to spiritual realization.

## Practical Application for Meditation

1. **Contemplate death** — Not morbidly, but as a meditation. If the body falls away, what remains? Can you find something in your direct experience that was never born and can never die?
   Affirm: *Na me mṛtyuśhaṅkā* — "I have no fear of death, because I was never born."

2. **Release identity labels** — Notice how the mind constantly narrates: "I am a [profession], a [nationality], a [caste], a [role]." Each label is a limitation placed on the limitless.
   Affirm: *Na me jātibhedaḥ* — "No label defines the Self."

3. **Sit with the absence of relationships** — Not as loneliness, but as freedom. Imagine, just for a moment, that you have no parents, no family, no friends, no teachers, no students. What remains? A vast, open awareness — full, not empty.
   Affirm: *Na bandhur na mitram gurur naiva śhiṣhyaḥ* — "All roles are appearances in consciousness. I am the consciousness itself."

4. **Rest in boundless identity**: *Chidānanda rūpaḥ Śhivo'ham Śhivo'ham.*

## Summary

Verse V addresses the two deepest anchors of human identity: **mortality** and **relationships**. Śaṅkara declares the Self free from the fear of death — because the unborn cannot die — and free from caste distinction — because the formless has no social category. He then dissolves all relational identity: father, mother, birth, family, friends, guru, and disciple. These are roles of the body-mind within the empirical world; the Self, being non-dual, stands prior to all relationship.

What emerges from this dissolution is not loneliness but the recognition of the Self's infinite, undivided nature — **Chidānanda rūpaḥ** — where there is no "other" to be separate from, and therefore no possibility of loneliness, loss, or death.
