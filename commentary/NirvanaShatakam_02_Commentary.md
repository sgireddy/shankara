# निर्वाण षट्कम् — Nirvana Shatakam — Verse II: Detailed Commentary

## ॐ

---

## The Verse (श्लोकः)

### Sanskrit (संस्कृतम्)

> न च प्राण संज्ञो न वै पञ्चवायुः
> न वा सप्तधातुर्न वा पञ्चकोशः ।
> न वाक्पाणिपादौ न चोपस्थपायू
> चिदानन्द रूपः शिवोऽहं शिवोऽहम् ॥

### Telugu (తెలుగు)

> న చ ప్రాణ సంజ్ఞో న వై పంచవాయుః
> న వా సప్తధాతుర్న వా పంచకోశః ।
> న వాక్పాణిపాదౌ న చోపస్థపాయూ
> చిదానంద రూపః శివోఽహం శివోఽహమ్ ॥

### Transliteration

> Na cha prāṇa saṁjño na vai panchavāyuḥ
> Na vā saptadhātur na vā panchakośhaḥ
> Na vākpāṇipādau na chopasthapāyū
> Chidānanda rūpaḥ Śhivo'ham Śhivo'ham

### English Translation

> I am not the vital breath. I am not the five airs.
> I am not the seven bodily tissues, nor the five sheaths.
> I am not the voice, not the hands, not the feet, nor the organs.
> I am of the nature of Consciousness-Bliss. I am Shiva. I am Shiva.

---

## Theme: Stripping the Body (శరీరము యొక్క నిరాకరణ)

Having negated the inner psychological apparatus, the sense organs, and the gross elements in Verse I, Śaṅkara now turns to the **vital energies, the bodily constituents, the subtle-body sheaths, and the organs of action**. This verse dismantles identification with the *prāṇamaya* (vital), *physiological*, and *functional* dimensions of embodied existence. If Verse I stripped the mind and the senses, Verse II strips the **living, breathing, acting body** in its entirety.

---

## Pāda-by-Pāda (Line-by-Line) Commentary

### Pāda 1: *Na cha prāṇa saṁjño na vai panchavāyuḥ*

**"I am not known as the vital breath. I am not the five airs."**

#### Prāṇa — The Vital Breath

*Prāṇa* in its general sense refers to the vital life-force that animates the body. It is the most intimate marker of biological life — when prāṇa departs, we say the person has "died." For this reason, many traditions identify the Self with prāṇa: "I am alive, therefore I *am* this life-force." Śaṅkara negates this directly.

The word **saṁjñaḥ** (known as, identified with) is significant. Śaṅkara is not denying that prāṇa exists or functions in the body; he is denying that the Self is *known through* or *defined by* prāṇa. The Self is not "the living one" — it is the awareness that knows both life and death.

#### The Pañcha Vāyu (Five Vital Airs)

Vedāntic and Yogic physiology describes five functional modifications of prāṇa, each governing a specific bodily process:

| Sanskrit | Transliteration | Function | Location |
|---|---|---|---|
| प्राण (prāṇa) | Prāṇa | Inhalation, intake of energy | Heart / chest region |
| अपान (apāna) | Apāna | Exhalation, elimination, downward movement | Lower abdomen |
| व्यान (vyāna) | Vyāna | Distribution, circulation throughout the body | Pervades the entire body |
| उदान (udāna) | Udāna | Upward movement, speech, departure at death | Throat region |
| समान (samāna) | Samāna | Digestion, assimilation, balancing | Navel region |

These five vāyus regulate every physiological function — breathing, digestion, circulation, excretion, and the final departure of the soul. Together, they constitute the *prāṇamaya kośa* (vital sheath). By saying "I am not the five airs," Śaṅkara negates the entire physiological engine that sustains the body.

#### Philosophical Significance

The Chāndogya Upaniṣad (1.11.5) records a famous debate among the senses and prāṇa over which is supreme. Prāṇa wins — when it threatens to leave, all other faculties begin to collapse. This might tempt one to equate the Self with prāṇa. But Śaṅkara, following the deeper teaching of the Upaniṣads, clarifies: prāṇa is supreme among the *instruments*, but it is still an instrument. The Self is the master, not the tool.

---

### Pāda 2: *Na vā saptadhātur na vā panchakośhaḥ*

**"I am not the seven bodily tissues, nor the five sheaths."**

#### Sapta Dhātu — The Seven Bodily Tissues

Āyurveda (the traditional Indian science of medicine) classifies the physical body into seven constituent tissues (*dhātus*), each nourishing the next in sequence:

| # | Sanskrit | Transliteration | Tissue | Function |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| 1 | रसः (rasaḥ) | Rasa | Plasma / Lymph | Nourishment, hydration |
| 2 | रक्तम् (raktam) | Rakta | Blood | Oxygenation, vitalization |
| 3 | मांसम् (māṁsam) | Māṁsa | Muscle | Structure, movement |
| 4 | मेदस् (medas) | Medas | Fat / Adipose | Lubrication, insulation |
| 5 | अस्थि (asthi) | Asthi | Bone | Framework, support |
| 6 | मज्जा (majjā) | Majjā | Marrow / Nerve | Filling bones, nervous function |
| 7 | शुक्रम् (śhukram) | Śhukra | Reproductive tissue | Procreation, vitality |

These seven dhātus constitute the entire material substance of the physical body. By negating them, Śaṅkara declares: "I am not this flesh and blood. I am not any combination of physical matter."

#### Pañcha Kośa — The Five Sheaths

The Taittirīya Upaniṣad (2.1–2.5) teaches that the Self is enveloped by five concentric sheaths (*kośas*), like the layers of an onion:

| # | Sanskrit | Transliteration | Sheath | Composed Of |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| 1 | अन्नमय कोश | Annamaya Kośa | Food sheath | Physical body, the five elements |
| 2 | प्राणमय कोश | Prāṇamaya Kośa | Vital sheath | Five prāṇas and the organs of action |
| 3 | मनोमय कोश | Manomaya Kośa | Mental sheath | Mind (*manas*) and the five sense organs |
| 4 | विज्ञानमय कोश | Vijñānamaya Kośa | Intellectual sheath | Intellect (*buddhi*) and the sense organs |
| 5 | आनन्दमय कोश | Ānandamaya Kośa | Bliss sheath | Causal ignorance (*avidyā*) and its joy |

The kośa model is the Upaniṣadic equivalent of a guided meditation: at each layer, the student is told, "This is not the Self — go deeper." The Self (*Ātman*) is the witness behind all five sheaths — untouched by any of them.

By negating *all five kośas in a single phrase*, Śaṅkara achieves something remarkable: he collapses the entire Taittirīya teaching into four words. The physical body (Annamaya), the vital body (Prāṇamaya), the mental body (Manomaya), the intellectual body (Vijñānamaya), and even the bliss body (Ānandamaya) — none of these is the Self.

#### The Ānandamaya Kośa Controversy

The negation of the *Ānandamaya Kośa* is particularly significant. Some schools (notably the Mīmāṃsakas and some Vaiṣṇava commentators) argued that the bliss sheath *is* Brahman, since the Taittirīya Upaniṣad seems to culminate there. Śaṅkara refuted this vigorously in his Brahma Sūtra Bhāṣya (1.1.12–19), arguing that even the bliss sheath is a *modification* (*vikāra*) and therefore cannot be the unconditioned Absolute. The true Self lies *beyond* even bliss-as-an-experience.

---

### Pāda 3: *Na vākpāṇipādau na chopasthapāyū*

**"I am not the voice, not the hands, not the feet, nor the organs of generation and excretion."**

#### The Karmendriyas (Five Organs of Action)

While Verse I negated the *jñānendriyas* (organs of perception — ears, tongue, nose, eyes, and skin), this line negates the *karmendriyas* — the five organs of action through which the body interacts with and acts upon the world:

| Sanskrit | Transliteration | Organ | Faculty |
|---|---|---|---|
| वाक् (vāk) | Vāk | Voice / Speech organ | Speaking (*vachana*) |
| पाणि (pāṇi) | Pāṇi | Hands | Grasping (*ādāna*) |
| पादौ (pādau) | Pāda | Feet | Movement (*gamana*) |
| उपस्थ (upastha) | Upastha | Organ of generation | Procreation (*ānanda*) |
| पायू (pāyū) | Pāyu | Organ of excretion | Elimination (*visarga*) |

Together with the five jñānendriyas (negated in Verse I), these ten indriyas constitute the complete set of instruments through which the jīva (individual soul) interacts with the phenomenal world.

#### The Body as Instrument

The cumulative effect of this line, combined with Verse I's negations, is total: the Self is not any organ of perception, nor any organ of action. The body is revealed as a **complex instrument** — like a chariot (as described in the Kaṭha Upaniṣad 1.3.3-4) — and the Self is the **master** who rides it, not any part of the chariot itself.

The Kaṭha Upaniṣad's famous chariot metaphor:
- The **Ātman** is the master of the chariot
- The **body** is the chariot
- The **buddhi** is the charioteer
- The **manas** is the reins
- The **indriyas** are the horses
- The **sense objects** are the roads

Śaṅkara, in this verse, negates the chariot, the reins, the horses — everything except the master.

---

### Pāda 4 (Refrain): *Chidānanda rūpaḥ Śhivo'ham Śhivo'ham*

**"I am of the nature of Consciousness-Bliss. I am Shiva. I am Shiva."**

The refrain returns with even greater force after the second round of negation. Having stripped away not only the mind and senses (Verse I) but now also the vital airs, the physical constituents, the subtle sheaths, and the organs of action, the question intensifies: *"If I am none of these, then what am I?"*

The answer remains the same — unchanging, unshakable: **Chidānanda rūpaḥ**. The Self does not change based on what is negated. Whether you strip away thought or breath, muscle or sheath, speech or movement — what remains is always the same pure Consciousness-Bliss. This constancy of the refrain is itself a teaching: the Self is *nirvikāra* — changeless.

## Structural Analysis

### The Three Bodies (Śarīra Traya)

Vedānta teaches that the jīva operates through three bodies:

| Body | Sanskrit | Composed Of | Negated In |
|---|---|---|---|
| Gross Body (*Sthūla Śarīra*) | स्थूल शरीर | Five elements, seven dhātus | Verse I (elements), Verse II (dhātus) |
| Subtle Body (*Sūkṣma Śarīra*) | सूक्ष्म शरीर | Five prāṇas, ten indriyas, manas, buddhi | Verse I (mind, jñānendriyas), Verse II (prāṇas, karmendriyas) |
| Causal Body (*Kāraṇa Śarīra*) | कारण शरीर | Avidyā (primal ignorance) | Implicit in pañcakośa negation (Ānandamaya) |

By the end of Verse II, all three bodies have been negated. The Self stands revealed as the *Turīya* — the "Fourth" — beyond waking, dreaming, and deep sleep, beyond gross, subtle, and causal.

### Complementary Structure of Verses I and II

| Verse I Negates | Verse II Negates |
|---|---|
| Antaḥkaraṇa (mind, intellect, ego, memory) | Prāṇa and five vāyus |
| Jñānendriyas (organs of perception) | Karmendriyas (organs of action) |
| Pañca Mahābhūtas (five elements) | Sapta Dhātu (seven tissues) + Pañca Kośa (five sheaths) |

Together, the two verses form a complete negation of everything the embodied individual takes itself to be — psychologically, physiologically, and structurally.

## Philosophical Connections

### The Prāṇa Teaching in the Upaniṣads

The Praśna Upaniṣad is dedicated almost entirely to the glory of Prāṇa. In Question II, Sage Pippalāda declares that Prāṇa is the greatest among all faculties. In Question III, the five vāyus are described in detail. A student encountering these teachings might conclude: "Prāṇa is supreme — it must be the Self."

Śaṅkara's negation here is a corrective: Prāṇa is glorious *within* the realm of instruments, but it is not the Self. The Muṇḍaka Upaniṣad (2.1.3) distinguishes clearly:

> *Etasmāt jāyate prāṇo manaḥ sarvendriyāṇi cha*
> "From this [Brahman] are born prāṇa, mind, and all the senses."

What is *born from* Brahman cannot *be* Brahman. Prāṇa is an effect; the Self is the cause.

### Kośa Viveka — Discrimination of the Sheaths

The *pañcakośa viveka* (discrimination of the five sheaths) is one of the primary practices taught in Śaṅkara's Vivekacūḍāmaṇi. The practitioner contemplates each sheath and sees that it is: (a) changing, (b) insentient in itself, and (c) an object of awareness. Since the Self is the unchanging, sentient subject, it cannot be any of the sheaths.

Śaṅkara writes in the Vivekacūḍāmaṇi (verse 154):

> *Pañcakośādi-yogena tat-tan-mayeva bhāsate / Śuddha-ātmā nīla-vastādi-yogena sphaṭiko yathā*
> "Due to association with the five sheaths, the pure Self appears to be identical with them — just as a crystal appears to be red, blue, etc., due to association with colored cloth."

The Self merely *reflects* through the sheaths; it does not become them.

## Practical Application for Meditation

1. **Observe the breath** — the rise and fall of prāṇa. Notice that *you* are aware of the breath, but the breath is not aware of you.
   Affirm: *Na cha prāṇa saṁjño* — "I am not this breath. I am the one who watches it."

2. **Contemplate the body** — its flesh, blood, bones, and tissues. Recognize that these are material substances, constantly changing and ultimately perishable.
   Affirm: *Na vā saptadhātuḥ* — "I am not these tissues. I existed before them and will exist without them."

3. **Peel away the sheaths** — the physical body, the vital energy, the thinking mind, the discerning intellect, even the feeling of bliss in deep sleep. At each layer, ask: "Am I this?" and find: "No — I am the one who knows this."
   Affirm: *Na vā panchakośhaḥ* — "I am not any sheath. I am the Self within all sheaths."

4. **Notice your actions** — speaking, grasping, walking. Recognize that these are functions of organs, not the essence of who you are.
   Affirm: *Na vākpāṇipādau na chopasthapāyū* — "Actions happen through the body; I am the silent witness."

5. **Rest in the recognition**: *Chidānanda rūpaḥ Śhivo'ham Śhivo'ham.*

## Summary

In this second verse, Śaṅkara completes the negation begun in Verse I by stripping away the **vital energies** (prāṇa and the five vāyus), the **physical body** (seven dhātus), the **entire sheath structure** (five kośas), and the **organs of action** (speech, hands, feet, and the generative and excretory organs). Together, Verses I and II constitute a total negation of the three bodies — gross, subtle, and causal — and all their components.

What remains after this comprehensive stripping is not a void but the radiant fullness of **Chidānanda** — Consciousness-Bliss — the ever-present Self that was never actually covered, only apparently so.
