# निर्वाण षट्कम् — Nirvana Shatakam — Verse III: Detailed Commentary

## ॐ

---

## The Verse (श्लोकः)

### Sanskrit (संस्कृतम्)

> न मे द्वेषरागौ न मे लोभमोहौ
> मदो नैव मे नैव मात्सर्यभावः ।
> न धर्मो न चार्थो न कामो न मोक्षः
> चिदानन्द रूपः शिवोऽहं शिवोऽहम् ॥

### Telugu (తెలుగు)

> న మే ద్వేషరాగౌ న మే లోభమోహౌ
> మదో నైవ మే నైవ మాత్సర్యభావః ।
> న ధర్మో న చార్థో న కామో న మోక్షః
> చిదానంద రూపః శివోఽహం శివోఽహమ్ ॥

### Transliteration

> Na me dveṣharāgau na me lobhamohau
> Mado naiva me naiva mātsaryabhāvaḥ
> Na dharmo na chārtho na kāmo na mokshaḥ
> Chidānanda rūpaḥ Śhivo'ham Śhivo'ham

### English Translation

> I have no hatred or passion. I have no greed or delusion.
> I have no pride, nor any jealousy.
> I have no dharma (duty), no artha (wealth), no kama (desire), no moksha (liberation).
> I am of the nature of Consciousness-Bliss. I am Shiva. I am Shiva.

---

## Theme: Stripping Desire and Morality (కోరికలు మరియు ధర్మాల నిరాకరణ)

Verses I and II negated the **instruments** — the mind, senses, body, breath, and sheaths. Now, in Verse III, Śaṅkara shifts to negating the **emotional and moral content** that flows through those instruments. This is a far more radical and unsettling negation. Most seekers can accept "I am not the body." Far fewer can accept "I have no virtue, no sin, no desire — not even for liberation." Yet this is precisely the teaching: the Self transcends not only the apparatus of experience but the entire landscape of experience itself — including the most cherished goals of religious life.

---

## Pāda-by-Pāda (Line-by-Line) Commentary

### Pāda 1: *Na me dveṣharāgau na me lobhamohau*

**"I have no hatred or passion. I have no greed or delusion."**

#### The Ṣaḍripu — The Six Inner Enemies (Part 1)

Hindu psychology identifies six fundamental afflictions (*ṣaḍripu* — "six enemies") that bind the jīva to saṃsāra (the cycle of birth and death). Lines 1 and 2 of this verse systematically negate all six:

| Sanskrit | Transliteration | Enemy | Nature |
|---|---|---|---|
| रागः (rāgaḥ) | Rāga | Passion / Attachment | Craving for what is pleasant; clinging to what one has |
| द्वेषः (dveṣhaḥ) | Dveṣha | Hatred / Aversion | Repulsion from what is unpleasant; pushing away what one dislikes |
| लोभः (lobhaḥ) | Lobha | Greed | Insatiable wanting; the desire for more and more |
| मोहः (mohaḥ) | Moha | Delusion | Confusion about what is real and unreal; the root ignorance |

#### Rāga and Dveṣha — The Primary Pair

Rāga (attachment) and dveṣha (aversion) are the two most fundamental emotional movements of the mind. The Bhagavad Gītā (3.34) identifies them as the great enemies on the path:

> *Indriyasyendriyasyārthe rāgadveṣhau vyavasthitau / Tayor na vaśham āgachchhet tau hyasya paripanthinau*
> "Attachment and aversion lie in wait for every sense in relation to its object. One should not come under their sway, for they are one's enemies on the path."

Every mental movement is colored by one or both: either we are drawn toward something (rāga) or repelled by something (dveṣha). Together, they create the oscillating, restless quality of the mind. Śaṅkara declares the Self free from both — not through suppression, but through its very nature. The Self, being infinite and complete, has nothing to gain (hence no rāga) and nothing to lose (hence no dveṣha).

#### Lobha and Moha — The Amplifiers

Lobha (greed) is rāga amplified — not merely wanting, but wanting without limit. Moha (delusion) is the cognitive fog that makes the entire cycle possible. It is moha that makes one mistake the body for the Self, the transient for the permanent, the painful for the pleasurable. The Bhagavad Gītā (2.63) traces the chain of spiritual destruction:

> *Krodhād bhavati sammohaḥ sammohāt smṛti-vibhramaḥ*
> "From anger comes delusion; from delusion, the wandering of memory."

Moha is the root; rāga, dveṣha, and lobha are its branches.

---

### Pāda 2: *Mado naiva me naiva mātsaryabhāvaḥ*

**"I have no pride, nor any jealousy."**

#### The Ṣaḍripu — The Six Inner Enemies (Part 2)

| Sanskrit | Transliteration | Enemy | Nature |
|---|---|---|---|
| मदः (madaḥ) | Mada | Pride / Arrogance | Intoxication with one's own qualities — beauty, wealth, knowledge, power |
| मात्सर्यम् (mātsaryam) | Mātsarya | Jealousy / Envy | Resentment at another's good fortune; inability to tolerate others' success |

#### Mada — The Intoxication of the Ego

*Mada* literally means "intoxication." It is the self-congratulatory inflation that arises when the ego identifies with some positive quality: "I am learned," "I am wealthy," "I am powerful." The Mahābhārata lists *mada* among the greatest obstacles to wisdom. The irony of mada is that the very qualities one takes pride in — knowledge, beauty, strength — are all properties of the body-mind complex, which Śaṅkara has already shown is not the Self.

#### Mātsarya — The Poison of Comparison

*Mātsarya* is the inability to bear the prosperity or virtues of another. It arises from the delusion that the world is a zero-sum game — that another's gain is my loss. The Self, being infinite and non-dual, has no "other" to compare with; hence mātsarya is structurally impossible at the level of Ātman.

#### Completion of the Ṣaḍripu Negation

With these six negated — kāma (implied in rāga), krodha (implied in dveṣha), lobha, moha, mada, and mātsarya — Śaṅkara frees the Self from the entire emotional architecture of saṃsāric existence. These are not merely psychological states to be managed; they are revealed as having no fundamental connection to the Self at all.

---

### Pāda 3: *Na dharmo na chārtho na kāmo na mokshaḥ*

**"I have no dharma (duty), no artha (wealth), no kama (desire), no moksha (liberation)."**

#### The Puruṣārthas — The Four Goals of Human Life

This is the most audacious line in the entire Nirvana Shatakam, and perhaps one of the most radical statements in all of Vedāntic literature. Śaṅkara negates the *puruṣārthas* — the four traditionally sanctioned goals of human life:

| Sanskrit | Transliteration | Goal | Description |
|---|---|---|---|
| धर्मः (dharmaḥ) | Dharma | Duty / Righteousness | Moral and ethical conduct; cosmic order; one's duty according to station in life |
| अर्थः (arthaḥ) | Artha | Wealth / Prosperity | Material security, economic well-being, political power |
| कामः (kāmaḥ) | Kāma | Desire / Pleasure | Sensory and aesthetic enjoyment, emotional fulfillment, love |
| मोक्षः (mokshaḥ) | Mokṣha | Liberation | Freedom from the cycle of birth and death; the supreme spiritual goal |

#### The Shock of Negating Dharma

The negation of dharma is shocking within the Hindu context, where dharma is considered the very foundation of cosmic order. The Mahābhārata declares: *Dharmo rakṣhati rakṣhitaḥ* — "Dharma protects those who protect dharma." How can the Self be beyond dharma?

Śaṅkara's answer: dharma belongs to the *empirical* realm (*vyāvahārika*). It governs the conduct of the body-mind in the world of transactions. But the Self is not a transacting entity. It neither acts nor refrains from acting. As the Bhagavad Gītā (3.17) states about the Self-realized person:

> *Ātmanyeva cha santuṣhṭasya tasya kāryam na vidyate*
> "For one who rejoices in the Self alone, there remains no duty to perform."

#### The Supreme Paradox: Negating Mokṣha

Even more startling is the negation of *mokṣha* — liberation itself. If the entire purpose of Vedānta is to achieve mokṣha, how can Śaṅkara deny it?

The answer reveals the deepest teaching of Advaita: **Mokṣha is not something to be attained.** The Self is *already* free — it was never bound. Bondage is an illusion (*adhyāsa*) superimposed by ignorance. When ignorance is removed, one does not *gain* liberation; one *recognizes* that liberation was always the case. As Śaṅkara writes in the Vivekacūḍāmaṇi (verse 570):

> *Bandhomokṣhau na me, na me* — "Bondage and liberation do not exist for me."

To desire mokṣha is still to be caught in a desire — the subtlest and last desire, but a desire nonetheless. True freedom is the recognition that there is no one who was ever bound, and therefore no liberation needed.

#### The Puruṣārtha Framework Transcended

The four puruṣārthas represent the totality of human aspiration. By negating all four, Śaṅkara declares that the Self is beyond the entire human project. It has no agenda, no goal, no direction, no lack. It is *pūrṇa* — complete — as the Iśā Upaniṣad opens:

> *Pūrṇam adaḥ pūrṇam idam pūrṇāt pūrṇam udachyate / Pūrṇasya pūrṇam ādāya pūrṇam evāvaśhiṣhyate*
> "That is whole. This is whole. From the whole, the whole arises. When the whole is taken from the whole, the whole alone remains."

---

### Pāda 4 (Refrain): *Chidānanda rūpaḥ Śhivo'ham Śhivo'ham*

**"I am of the nature of Consciousness-Bliss. I am Shiva. I am Shiva."**

After the most psychologically and spiritually challenging negation yet — the stripping of emotions, morality, and even the desire for God — the refrain lands with the weight of absolute certainty. The Self is not diminished by the absence of emotions, goals, or duties. It is *Chidānanda* — fullness itself.

The refrain after this verse carries a particular flavor of **freedom** (*svātantrya*). The first two verses freed the Self from its instruments. This verse frees it from its *motivations*. What remains is motivation-less, agenda-less, pure being — and paradoxically, this is not emptiness but the deepest possible bliss.

## Philosophical Connections

### Relation to the Bhagavad Gītā's Sthitaprajña

The Bhagavad Gītā, Chapter 2 (verses 55–72), describes the *sthitaprajña* — the person of "steady wisdom." This description closely mirrors Verse III:

- *Prajahāti yadā kāmān sarvān pārtha manogatān* (2.55) — "When one abandons all desires of the mind..."
- *Duḥkheṣhu anudvignamanāḥ sukheṣhu vigataspṛhaḥ / Vītarāgabhayakrodhaḥ* (2.56) — "Undisturbed in sorrow, without craving for pleasure, free from passion, fear, and anger..."

The sthitaprajña has no rāga, no dveṣha, no lobha, no moha, no mada, no mātsarya. The verse describes the *lived experience* of what Śaṅkara declares as the *ontological truth* of the Self.

### Naiṣkarmya Siddhi — The Perfection of Non-Action

Śaṅkara's direct disciple, Sureśvara, wrote the *Naiṣkarmya Siddhi* ("The Attainment of Non-Action"), which elaborates on exactly this point: the Self is inherently free from all action, all duty, all purpose. The concept of *naiṣkarmya* (non-action) does not mean laziness or withdrawal — it means recognizing that the Self was never the doer.

### Beyond Māyā's Emotional Architecture

The ṣaḍripus are not random afflictions — they are the structural pillars of *māyā's* emotional world. Rāga creates the forward pull; dveṣha creates the backward push; lobha creates hunger; moha creates confusion; mada creates inflation; mātsarya creates division. Together, they keep the jīva perpetually in motion — chasing, fleeing, hoarding, comparing. By negating all six, Śaṅkara pulls out the foundation of saṃsāric experience.

## Practical Application for Meditation

1. **Observe emotions as they arise** — attraction, aversion, greed, confusion, pride, envy. Do not suppress them or indulge them. Simply notice: "This is arising in the mind. I am the awareness that sees it."
   Affirm: *Na me dveṣharāgau na me lobhamohau* — "These emotions are not me."

2. **Examine your deepest motivations** — What drives you? Duty? Security? Pleasure? Even spiritual liberation? Notice that every goal implies a lack — something you don't yet have.
   Affirm: *Na dharmo na chārtho na kāmo na mokshaḥ* — "The Self has no lack. It seeks nothing."

3. **Sit with the paradox** — "If I don't need mokṣha, why am I meditating?" Allow the mind to be confused. The confusion is the mind's; the Self is already clear.
   Affirm: *Chidānanda rūpaḥ Śhivo'ham Śhivo'ham* — "I am already what I seek."

## Summary

Verse III marks a pivotal turning point in the Nirvana Shatakam. Having negated the body-mind apparatus in Verses I and II, Śaṅkara now negates the **entire emotional and aspirational life** of the individual — the six inner enemies (rāga, dveṣha, lobha, moha, mada, mātsarya) and the four goals of human life (dharma, artha, kāma, mokṣha). This is the most philosophically daring verse in the hymn, for it declares the Self free not only from sin but from virtue, not only from bondage but from liberation itself.

The Self does not need to be purified, improved, or freed. It is, always was, and always will be: **Chidānanda rūpaḥ Śhivo'ham Śhivo'ham.**
