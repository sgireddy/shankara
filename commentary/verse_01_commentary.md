# निर्वाण षट्कम् — Nirvana Shatakam — Verse I: Detailed Commentary

## The Verse (श्लोकः)

### Sanskrit (संस्कृतम्)

> मनो बुद्ध्यहंकार चित्तानि नाहं
> न च श्रोत्र जिह्वे न च घ्राण नेत्रे ।
> न च व्योम भूमिः न तेजो न वायुः
> चिदानन्द रूपः शिवोऽहं शिवोऽहम् ॥

### Telugu (తెలుగు)

> మనో బుద్ధ్యహంకార చిత్తాని నాహం
> న చ శ్రోత్ర జిహ్వే న చ ఘ్రాణ నేత్రే ।
> న చ వ్యోమ భూమిః న తేజో న వాయుః
> చిదానంద రూపః శివోఽహం శివోఽహమ్ ॥

### Transliteration

> Mano buddhyahankāra chittāni nāham
> Na cha shrotra jihve na cha ghrāṇa netre
> Na cha vyoma bhūmiḥ na tejo na vāyuḥ
> Chidānanda rūpaḥ Śhivo'ham Śhivo'ham

### English Translation

> I am not the mind, not the intellect, not the ego, not the memory.
> I am not the ears, nor the tongue, nor the nose, nor the eyes.
> I am not space, not earth, not fire, not air.
> I am of the nature of Consciousness-Bliss. I am Shiva. I am Shiva.

---

## Theme: Stripping the Mind, the Senses, and the Elements

The first verse of the Nirvana Shatakam establishes the foundational method of the entire hymn: **neti-neti** (नेति नेति — "not this, not this"), the great Vedāntic technique of negation. Śrī Ādi Śaṅkarācārya begins by systematically denying the Self's identity with the internal instruments of cognition (*antaḥkaraṇa*), the sense organs (*jñānendriya*), and the five gross elements (*pañca mahābhūta*). What remains after this stripping away is the pure, unconditioned Self — *Chidānanda rūpaḥ*, the very nature of Consciousness-Bliss.

---

## Background and Context

### The Legend of Composition

According to traditional accounts, Śaṅkara composed this hymn as a young boy of eight while seeking his future guru, Śrī Govindapāda, on the banks of the Narmadā river. When the sage asked, *"Ko'si?"* — "Who are you?" — the boy did not answer with his name, lineage, or caste. Instead, he answered with these six verses, each ending with the thundering refrain: **चिदानन्द रूपः शिवोऽहं शिवोऽहम्** — "I am of the nature of Consciousness-Bliss. I am Shiva. I am Shiva." The question *"Who are you?"* is thus the seed of the entire poem, and the answer is the direct experience of Advaita — non-duality.

### Place in Advaita Vedānta

The Nirvana Shatakam is a *prakaraṇa grantha* (introductory treatise) — a short, accessible doorway into the vast edifice of Advaita philosophy. It condenses the teaching of the Upaniṣads (especially the Māṇḍūkya and the Bṛhadāraṇyaka) into six verses that can be chanted, memorized, and meditated upon. The first verse targets the most immediate layers of misidentification: the mind, the senses, and the physical world.

## Pāda-by-Pāda (Line-by-Line) Commentary

### Pāda 1: *Mano buddhyahankāra chittāni nāham*

**"I am not the mind, not the intellect, not the ego, not the memory."**

#### The Antaḥkaraṇa Chatuṣṭaya (The Fourfold Internal Organ)

Śaṅkara targets the four components of the *antaḥkaraṇa* (internal instrument), which together constitute our entire inner psychological world:

| Sanskrit | Transliteration | Meaning | Function |
|---|---|---|---|
| मनः (manaḥ) | manas | Mind | *Saṅkalpa-vikalpa* — thinking, doubting, oscillating between options. The mind raises questions and possibilities. |
| बुद्धिः (buddhiḥ) | buddhi | Intellect | *Niśchaya* — determination, decision-making, discernment. The buddhi resolves what the manas presents. |
| अहंकारः (ahankāraḥ) | ahankāra | Ego | *Abhimāna* — the sense of "I" and "mine," the feeling of individual ownership and doership. |
| चित्तम् (chittam) | chitta | Memory / mind-stuff | *Smṛti* — recollection, the storehouse of impressions (*saṃskāra*) accumulated over lifetimes. |

These four are not separate organs but **four functional modes of a single subtle instrument**. Together, they create the entire subjective experience — thinking, deciding, claiming ownership, and remembering. For the ordinary person, the Self is hopelessly entangled with these functions: "I think," "I decide," "I am the doer," "I remember."

Śaṅkara's negation cuts through all of this at once. **नाहम् (nāham)** — "I am not." The Self is not any of these functions. It is the **witness** (*sākṣī*) of all four — the unchanging awareness in which thinking, deciding, claiming, and remembering arise and dissolve.

#### Philosophical Significance

This negation addresses the subtlest and most tenacious form of misidentification. Most spiritual seekers can intellectually accept that they are not the physical body. Far fewer can accept that they are not their thoughts, their intelligence, their sense of self, or their memories. Yet Śaṅkara begins here — at the deepest layer — because the *antaḥkaraṇa* is the very instrument through which all other misidentifications are sustained.

In the Vivekacūḍāmaṇi (verse 183), Śaṅkara writes:

> *Manasā hi jagat sṛṣṭam* — "Through the mind alone is the world created."

If one can dis-identify from the mind, the entire projected world of suffering collapses.

---

### Pāda 2: *Na cha shrotra jihve na cha ghrāṇa netre*

**"I am not the ears, nor the tongue, nor the nose, nor the eyes."**

#### The Jñānendriyas (Organs of Perception)

Having negated the inner instrument, Śaṅkara now negates the *jñānendriyas* — the five sense organs through which the inner instrument receives information from the external world. Four are explicitly named here:

| Sanskrit | Transliteration | Sense Organ | Faculty |
|---|---|---|---|
| श्रोत्रम् (shrotram) | shrotra | Ears | Hearing (*śravaṇa*) |
| जिह्वा (jihvā) | jihvā | Tongue | Taste (*rasana*) |
| घ्राणम् (ghrāṇam) | ghrāṇa | Nose | Smell (*ghrāṇa*) |
| नेत्रे (netre) | netre | Eyes | Sight (*darśana*) |

The fifth sense — **tvak** (skin / touch) — is not explicitly mentioned here but is negated in the second verse of the hymn, where the *karmendriyas* (organs of action) are addressed. Some commentators hold that the dual form *netre* (eyes, in dual) and the inclusive force of *na cha* implicitly encompasses the entire set.

#### Why Negate the Senses?

The senses are the gates through which consciousness appears to flow outward into the world of objects. The Kaṭha Upaniṣad (2.1.1) describes them vividly:

> *Parāñci khāni vyatṛṇat svayambhūḥ tasmāt parāṅ paśyati nāntarātman*
> "The Self-existent Lord pierced the sense-openings outward; therefore, one looks outward and not within."

We habitually identify with what we see, hear, taste, smell, and touch. "I see the sunset" becomes "I am the seer." But the Self is not the seer — it is the awareness that illumines both the seer and the seen. The eyes do not see; they are merely instruments. The Self — pure consciousness — makes seeing possible.

---

### Pāda 3: *Na cha vyoma bhūmiḥ na tejo na vāyuḥ*

**"I am not space, not earth, not fire, not air."**

#### The Pañca Mahābhūtas (Five Great Elements)

Śaṅkara now negates the *pañca mahābhūtas* — the five gross elements that constitute the entire material universe:

| Sanskrit | Transliteration | Element | Quality (*Tanmātra*) |
|---|---|---|---|
| व्योम (vyoma) | vyoma | Space / Ether (*Ākāśa*) | Sound (*śabda*) |
| भूमिः (bhūmiḥ) | bhūmi | Earth (*Pṛthivī*) | Smell (*gandha*) |
| तेजः (tejaḥ) | tejas | Fire (*Agni*) | Form / Color (*rūpa*) |
| वायुः (vāyuḥ) | vāyu | Air (*Vāyu*) | Touch (*sparśa*) |

The fifth element — **Āpaḥ** (water) — is not mentioned in this verse but is negated in the second verse. Together, these five elements compose every material form — from the human body to the farthest galaxy.

#### The Scope of the Negation

By negating the five elements, Śaṅkara negates the entire physical universe. According to Sāṅkhya and Vedāntic cosmology, everything in the manifest world (*sthūla prapañca*) is a combination of these five elements in varying proportions. The human body itself is called *pañchabhautika śarīra* — the body made of five elements. So this line effectively says: **"I am not the body, and I am not the world."**

This is a radical statement. The materialist says, "I am my body in a physical world." Śaṅkara says the Self transcends both. Consciousness is not a product of matter — matter appears *within* consciousness.

---

### Pāda 4 (Refrain): *Chidānanda rūpaḥ Śhivo'ham Śhivo'ham*

**"I am of the nature of Consciousness-Bliss. I am Shiva. I am Shiva."**

This is the *dhruva-pāda* — the constant refrain that concludes all six verses. After the negation (*neti-neti*), this line provides the positive assertion (*iti-iti*) — the direct declaration of the Self's true nature.

#### Chidānanda (चिदानन्द) — Consciousness-Bliss

This compound word fuses two of the three defining attributes of Brahman as taught in the Upaniṣads:

- **Chit (चित्)** — Pure Consciousness, Awareness. Not consciousness *of* something, but consciousness itself — self-luminous, without subject or object.
- **Ānanda (आनन्द)** — Bliss, not as an emotion or feeling of happiness, but as the inherent fullness (*pūrṇatva*) of being. It is the absence of all want, the peace that remains when all desires are dissolved.

The third attribute — **Sat** (Existence) — is implicit. The Taittirīya Upaniṣad (2.1.1) declares: *Satyaṃ jñānam anantam brahma* — "Brahman is Truth, Knowledge, and Infinity." Śaṅkara, in choosing *Chidānanda*, emphasizes the experiential nature of realization: the Self is not an abstract concept but **living awareness and bliss**.

#### Rūpaḥ (रूपः) — Nature / Essence

*Rūpaḥ* here means "nature" or "essential form," not physical form. The compound *Chidānanda-rūpaḥ* means "one whose very nature *is* Consciousness-Bliss" — not one who *possesses* these qualities, but one who *is* these qualities.

#### Śivo'ham (शिवोऽहम्) — I am Shiva

*Śiva* here is not the name of a deity with a trident and a third eye. It is the adjective **śiva** — meaning "auspicious," "pure," "benevolent," "that which is eternally good." Śaṅkara is saying: "I am the Auspicious One. I am the pure, untainted, ever-blessed Self."

The word is repeated — **Śivo'ham Śhivo'ham** — for emphasis, certainty, and meditative absorption. This is not a tentative philosophical position; it is an ecstatic declaration of direct realization. The repetition also mirrors the Upaniṣadic practice of *āvṛtti* (repetition for emphasis), as seen in *śāntiḥ śāntiḥ śāntiḥ*.

## Structural Analysis

### The Three Layers of Negation

The verse follows a deliberate inward-to-outward structure:

1. **Antaḥkaraṇa** (internal organ) — the subtlest layer, the inner world of thought
2. **Jñānendriyas** (sense organs) — the interface between inner and outer
3. **Pañca Mahābhūtas** (five elements) — the grossest layer, the external material world

By negating from subtle to gross, Śaṅkara shows that the Self transcends the entire spectrum of phenomenal existence — from the most private thought to the most distant star. Nothing in the created world is the Self; yet the Self is the ground upon which all of it appears.

### Correspondence with the Pañcakośa Model

The negations in this verse correspond to the Taittirīya Upaniṣad's teaching of the five sheaths (*pañcakośa*):

| Negation in Verse 1 | Corresponding Kośa (Sheath) |
|---|---|
| Manas, Buddhi, Ahankāra, Chitta | *Manomaya Kośa* (mental sheath) and *Vijñānamaya Kośa* (intellectual sheath) |
| Shrotra, Jihvā, Ghrāṇa, Netre | *Prāṇamaya Kośa* (vital sheath, through which the senses operate) |
| Vyoma, Bhūmi, Tejas, Vāyu | *Annamaya Kośa* (food/physical sheath) |

The fifth sheath — *Ānandamaya Kośa* (bliss sheath) — is addressed in subsequent verses, where Śaṅkara negates even pleasure, pain, virtue, and sin.

## Philosophical Connections

### Relation to the Mahāvākyas

The refrain *Śivo'ham* is a direct echo of the great Vedāntic declarations (*mahāvākya*):

- **Aham Brahmāsmi** (अहं ब्रह्मास्मि) — "I am Brahman" (*Bṛhadāraṇyaka Upaniṣad* 1.4.10)
- **Tat Tvam Asi** (तत् त्वम् असि) — "You are That" (*Chāndogya Upaniṣad* 6.8.7)

*Śivo'ham* is Śaṅkara's poetic and devotional reformulation of *Aham Brahmāsmi* — the individual self declaring its identity with the Absolute.

### The Neti-Neti Method

The Bṛhadāraṇyaka Upaniṣad (2.3.6) teaches:

> *Atha neti neti, na hi etasmād iti netyanyat param asti*
> "Now the teaching: not this, not this. For there is nothing higher than this — 'not this.'"

The Self cannot be described positively through words, because every word denotes a limited object. The only way to point to the unlimited is to negate everything that is limited. This is exactly what verse 1 does — and what the remaining five verses continue to do with increasing scope.

### Sākṣī Bhāva — The Witness Stance

By saying "I am not the mind" and "I am not the senses," Śaṅkara implicitly establishes the **sākṣī** (witness) — the silent, unchanging awareness that observes all mental and sensory activity without being affected by it. The mind changes; the witness does not. The senses perceive objects; the witness illumines the senses themselves. This witness-consciousness is what is positively affirmed as *Chidānanda rūpaḥ*.

## Practical Application for Meditation

This verse is not merely a philosophical statement — it is a **meditation instruction**. The practitioner can use it as follows:

1. **Sit quietly** and observe the movements of the mind — thoughts arising and passing.
   Affirm: *Mano buddhyahankāra chittāni nāham* — "I am not these. I am the awareness watching them."

2. **Notice sensory input** — sounds, tastes, smells, sights.
   Affirm: *Na cha shrotra jihve na cha ghrāṇa netre* — "I am not these perceptions. I am the awareness in which they appear."

3. **Contemplate the body and the world** — the elements of which they are composed.
   Affirm: *Na cha vyoma bhūmiḥ na tejo na vāyuḥ* — "I am not this body, nor this world. I am the awareness that contains them."

4. **Rest in the recognition** of what remains when all identifications are released.
   Affirm: *Chidānanda rūpaḥ Śhivo'ham Śhivo'ham* — "I am pure Consciousness-Bliss. I am the Auspicious Self."

## Summary

In this opening verse, Śrī Ādi Śaṅkarācārya declares with absolute clarity: **the Self is not the psychological apparatus (mind, intellect, ego, memory), not the sensory apparatus (ears, tongue, nose, eyes), and not the material world (space, earth, fire, air)**. Having negated every possible object of identification, he affirms the Self's true nature as **Chidānanda** — Consciousness-Bliss — and declares: **Śivo'ham, Śivo'ham** — "I am Shiva, I am Shiva."

This verse is the gateway to the remaining five, each of which extends the negation to further layers of human experience — prāṇa, karma, pleasure, pain, death, dharma, relationships — until nothing remains but the radiant, unconditioned Self: ever-pure, ever-free, ever-blissful.
