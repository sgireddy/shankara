# निर्वाण षट्कम् — Nirvana Shatakam — Verse VI: Detailed Commentary

## The Verse (श्लोकः)

### Sanskrit (संस्कृतम्)

> अहं निर्विकल्पो निराकार रूपो
> विभुत्वाच्च सर्वत्र सर्वेन्द्रियाणाम् ।
> न चासंगतं नैव मुक्तिर्न मेयः
> चिदानन्द रूपः शिवोऽहं शिवोऽहम् ॥

### Telugu (తెలుగు)

> అహం నిర్వికల్పో నిరాకార రూపో
> విభుత్వాచ్చ సర్వత్ర సర్వేంద్రియాణామ్ ।
> న చాసంగతం నైవ ముక్తిర్న మేయః
> చిదానంద రూపః శివోఽహం శివోఽహమ్ ॥

### Transliteration

> Aham nirvikalpo nirākāra rūpo
> Vibhutvāchcha sarvatra sarvendriyāṇām
> Na chāsaṅgatam naiva muktir na meyaḥ
> Chidānanda rūpaḥ Śhivo'ham Śhivo'ham

### English Translation

> I am without thought-modification. I am formless.
> I pervade all the senses everywhere.
> I am neither attachment nor liberation, nor anything to be measured.
> I am of the nature of Consciousness-Bliss. I am Shiva. I am Shiva.

---

## Theme: The Final Declaration — The Self Revealed (అంతిమ ఘోషణ)

The five preceding verses were primarily *negations* — the systematic dismantling of every possible misidentification through the neti-neti method. In this culminating sixth verse, Śaṅkara shifts from negation to **positive affirmation**. For the first time, the Self is not merely described by what it is *not*, but by what it *is*: formless, thought-free, all-pervading, beyond attachment and liberation, beyond measurement. This is the verse of *sākṣātkāra* (direct realization) — the moment the seeker's inquiry resolves into the direct recognition of the Self's infinite nature.

---

## Pāda-by-Pāda (Line-by-Line) Commentary

### Pāda 1: *Aham nirvikalpo nirākāra rūpo*

**"I am without thought-modification. I am formless."**

#### Nirvikalpa — Beyond All Thought-Modification

*Nirvikalpa* (निर्विकल्पः) is one of the most important technical terms in Advaita Vedānta. It is composed of *nir-* (without) + *vikalpa* (thought-modification, mental construct, conceptual elaboration).

*Vikalpa* refers to the mind's ceaseless activity of:
- **Classifying**: "This is a tree, that is a person"
- **Comparing**: "This is better than that"
- **Narrating**: "I did this, then that happened"
- **Imagining**: "What if this occurs?"
- **Doubting**: "Is this real or not?"

All of these are *vikalpas* — mental modifications that create the apparent world of diversity. The Self is *nirvikalpa* — it exists prior to and independent of all mental constructs. It is not a thought, not the absence of thought, but the **ground of awareness** in which all thoughts arise and dissolve.

#### Connection to Nirvikalpa Samādhi

In Yogic and Vedāntic practice, *nirvikalpa samādhi* is the highest state of absorption — the state in which all mental modifications cease and pure consciousness alone remains. It is distinguished from *savikalpa samādhi* (absorption with thought-modifications still present):

| State | Sanskrit | Description |
|---|---|---|
| Savikalpa Samādhi | सविकल्प समाधि | Awareness of Brahman remains, but a subtle sense of "I am experiencing Brahman" persists — the tripuṭī is still faintly present |
| Nirvikalpa Samādhi | निर्विकल्प समाधि | All mental modifications cease. No subject-object distinction remains. Pure consciousness alone, self-luminous and undivided |

Śaṅkara declares: the Self's nature is *nirvikalpa* — not as an attainment of meditation, but as its **permanent, intrinsic condition**. Nirvikalpa samādhi reveals the Self; it does not create it. The Self was always nirvikalpa; it is only *avidyā* (ignorance) that projected vikalpas upon it.

#### Nirākāra Rūpa — Formless Form

*Nirākāra* (निराकारः) means "without form" (*nir-* + *ākāra*). Combined with *rūpa* (form/nature), the compound *nirākāra rūpa* creates a beautiful paradox: "the one whose form is formlessness" or "the one whose nature is to be without shape."

This echoes the Iśā Upaniṣad (verse 8):

> *Sa paryagāchchchhukram akāyam avraṇam asnāviram śhuddham apāpaviddham*
> "He [the Self] is all-pervading, radiant, bodiless, without wound, without sinew, pure, untouched by sin."

The Self is formless not because it is nothing, but because it is **everything** — unlimited by any particular shape, location, or dimension. A form implies boundaries; the boundless cannot have form.

---

### Pāda 2: *Vibhutvāchcha sarvatra sarvendriyāṇām*

**"By virtue of my all-pervading nature, I am everywhere, in all the senses."**

#### Vibhutva — All-Pervading Nature

*Vibhutva* (विभुत्वम्) means "the state of being all-pervading" or "omnipresence." It derives from *vibhu* (विभु) — "that which pervades everything."

The Self does not reside in one location — in the heart, in the brain, in a particular chakra. It pervades all of space and time, being the very ground of space and time. The Chāndogya Upaniṣad (3.14.3) describes Brahman:

> *Sarvagataḥ* — "All-pervading"
> *Sarva-rasaḥ* — "The essence of all"

#### Sarvatra Sarvendriyāṇām — Everywhere, in All the Senses

The phrase *sarvatra sarvendriyāṇām* means the Self pervades through and beyond all sense organs. This is a shift from the negations of Verses I and II. In those verses, Śaṅkara said "I am not the ears, not the eyes, not the senses." Now he reveals the deeper truth: **the Self is the consciousness that operates through every sense but is limited by none.**

The Bṛhadāraṇyaka Upaniṣad (3.7.23) describes the Self as the *Antaryāmī* (Inner Controller):

> *Yaḥ sarveṣhu bhūteṣhu tiṣhṭhan, sarvebhyo bhūtebhyo'ntaraḥ, yam sarvāṇi bhūtāni na viduḥ, yasya sarvāṇi bhūtāni śharīram, yaḥ sarvāṇi bhūtāni antaro yamayati, eṣha ta ātmā antaryāmy amṛtaḥ*
> "He who, dwelling in all beings, is within all beings, whom no being knows, whose body is all beings, who controls all beings from within — He is your Self, the Inner Controller, the Immortal."

The Self is the seeing in the eyes, the hearing in the ears, the thinking in the mind — yet it is not any of these organs or their functions. It is the **one consciousness** that makes all perception possible.

#### The Śvetāśvatara Upaniṣad's Vision

The Śvetāśvatara Upaniṣad (3.16) offers a related vision:

> *Sarvataḥ pāṇipādam tat sarvato'kṣhi-śhiro-mukham / Sarvataḥ śhrutimal loke sarvam āvṛtya tiṣhṭhati*
> "With hands and feet everywhere, with eyes, heads, and faces everywhere, with hearing everywhere — That exists, pervading everything in the universe."

This is the Self described in Verse VI: not localized in one body with two eyes and two ears, but the infinite awareness functioning through every eye, every ear, every sense organ in the universe.

---

### Pāda 3: *Na chāsaṅgatam naiva muktir na meyaḥ*

**"I am neither attachment nor liberation, nor anything to be measured."**

#### Asaṅgatam — Neither Attachment Nor Detachment

*Asaṅgata* (असंगत) means "attachment" or "association." The negation *na cha asaṅgatam* means "I am not [defined by] attachment." Combined with *naiva muktiḥ* (nor liberation), this creates a comprehensive negation of both sides of the spiritual coin:

| State | Sanskrit | Description |
|---|---|---|
| Bondage / Attachment | संग (saṅga) / बन्ध (bandha) | The state of being tied to the world through desire, karma, and identification |
| Liberation | मुक्ति (mukti) | Freedom from bondage; the goal of all spiritual practice |

Śaṅkara declares the Self is **neither bound nor free**. This echoes Verse III's negation of mokṣha and brings it to its ultimate philosophical expression. Bondage and liberation are a pair (*dvandva*) — one implies the other. To say "I am liberated" still implies there was a time of bondage. But the Self was never bound, so liberation is meaningless for it.

Gauḍapāda (Śaṅkara's grand-guru) states this with crystalline clarity in the Māṇḍūkya Kārikā (2.32):

> *Na nirodho na chotpattiḥ na baddho na cha sādhakaḥ / Na mumukṣhur na vai muktaḥ ityeṣhā paramārthatā*
> "There is no dissolution, no origination, no one bound, no spiritual aspirant, no one seeking liberation, no one liberated. This is the absolute truth."

This is the pinnacle of Advaita teaching — the *paramārtha darśhana* (absolute viewpoint) from which the entire drama of saṃsāra and mokṣha is seen as an appearance in the unchanging Self.

#### Na Meyaḥ — Not Measurable

*Meya* (मेयः) means "that which can be measured, known as an object, quantified." By declaring *na meyaḥ*, Śaṅkara asserts that the Self is not an object of any kind — not a thing to be grasped, measured, analyzed, or known *as an object*.

This negation addresses the fundamental epistemological question: "How can I know the Self?" The answer: you cannot know the Self the way you know an object, because the Self is the **knower** — the subject that makes all knowing possible. As Śaṅkara writes in the Upadeśa Sāhasrī:

> *Asmat-pratyaya-gocharaḥ...na meya-viṣhayaḥ*
> "That which is the referent of 'I'...is not an object of knowledge."

The Kena Upaniṣad (1.3) expresses this beautifully:

> *Na tatra chakṣhur gachchhati na vāg gachchhati no manaḥ / Na vidmo na vijānīmo yathaitad anuśhiṣhyāt*
> "The eye does not go there, nor speech, nor the mind. We do not know, we cannot understand, how one can teach about it."

And yet, the Self is not unknown. It is *self-luminous* (*svaprakāśha*) — known not as an object but as the very knowing itself. As the Kena Upaniṣad continues (2.4):

> *Pratibodha-viditam matam* — "It is known in every cognition."

The Self is the most intimately known reality — it is what you are — and yet it is never an object of knowledge. This paradox is dissolved only in direct realization, not in conceptual understanding.

---

### Pāda 4 (Refrain): *Chidānanda rūpaḥ Śhivo'ham Śhivo'ham*

**"I am of the nature of Consciousness-Bliss. I am Shiva. I am Shiva."**

In the final verse, the refrain is no longer merely the conclusion of a negation. It is the **culminating declaration of the entire hymn** — the answer that young Śaṅkara gave to the question *"Ko'si?"* ("Who are you?").

After six verses of systematic deconstruction — stripping away mind, body, senses, breath, sheaths, emotions, morals, goals, karma, religion, death, birth, and all relationships — what remains? Not a void. Not nothing. But the most real, most full, most alive reality there is: **Chidānanda** — Consciousness-Bliss.

The double declaration — *Śhivo'ham Śhivo'ham* — rings out not with uncertainty but with the absolute conviction of one who has directly seen. This is *aparokṣha anubhūti* — immediate, non-mediated experience of the Self. The teaching is complete. The question is answered. There is nothing more to say.

## Structural Analysis: The Culmination of the Hymn

### From Negation to Affirmation

| Verse | Method | What Is Negated/Affirmed |
|---|---|---|
| I | Negation | Mind, senses, elements |
| II | Negation | Prāṇa, body, sheaths, organs of action |
| III | Negation | Emotions (ṣaḍripu), the four puruṣārthas |
| IV | Negation | Karma, religion, the tripuṭī of experience |
| V | Negation | Death, birth, caste, relationships |
| **VI** | **Affirmation** | **Nirvikalpa, nirākāra, vibhu, beyond bondage/liberation, immeasurable** |

Verses I–V are the *neti-neti* (not this, not this). Verse VI is the *iti* (this is it) — the positive revelation of the Self's nature. The hymn moves from the via negativa to the via positiva, from stripping to revealing.

### The Complete Self-Portrait

Verse VI provides a five-fold positive characterization of the Self:

1. **Nirvikalpa** — Beyond all mental modification (transcends the *manomaya* and *vijñānamaya kośas*)
2. **Nirākāra** — Formless (transcends the *annamaya kośa* and all physical limitation)
3. **Vibhu** — All-pervading (transcends spatial limitation)
4. **Asaṅga-mukti-atīta** — Beyond bondage and liberation (transcends the entire saṃsāric framework)
5. **Ameya** — Immeasurable, not an object (transcends epistemological limitation)

Together, these five attributes describe a reality that is unlimited in every dimension — spatial, temporal, cognitive, and experiential.

## Philosophical Connections

### Turīya — The Fourth State

The Māṇḍūkya Upaniṣad's description of Turīya (verse 7) reads almost as a commentary on this verse:

> *Nāntaḥprajñam, na bahiṣhprajñam, nobhayataḥprajñam, na prajñāna-ghanam, na prajñam nāprajñam / Adṛṣhṭam avyavahāryam agrāhyam alakṣhaṇam achintyam avyapadeśyam ekātma-pratyayasāram prapañcopaśhamam śhāntam śhivam advaitam chaturtham manyante sa ātmā sa vijñeyaḥ*
> "It is not consciousness of the internal world, nor consciousness of the external world, nor consciousness of both, nor a mass of consciousness, nor consciousness, nor unconsciousness. It is unseen, beyond transaction, beyond grasp, without attributes, beyond thought, indescribable. It is the essence of the awareness of the one Self. It is the cessation of the manifold world. It is peaceful. It is auspicious (*śhivam*). It is non-dual. This they consider the Fourth. This is the Self. This is to be known."

Note the word *śhivam* in the Māṇḍūkya — the same word Śaṅkara uses in his refrain. The connection is deliberate: the Self of the Nirvana Shatakam is the Turīya of the Māṇḍūkya.

### Gauḍapāda's Asparśha Yoga

Gauḍapāda, in the Māṇḍūkya Kārikā (3.39), describes the state of the realized Self as *asparśha yoga* — "touchless union":

> *Asparśhayogo vai nāma sarvasattvahitaḥ sukhaḥ / Avivādo'viruddhaśhcha deśhitas tan namāmyaham*
> "That which is called 'contactless yoga' is the welfare of all beings, blissful, undisputed, non-contradictory. I bow to that which is thus taught."

*Asparśha* (untouched) resonates with the *asaṅga* (unattached) and *ameya* (immeasurable) of Verse VI. The Self is "touchless" — nothing touches it, changes it, or conditions it.

### The Aṣṭāvakra Gītā's Parallels

The Aṣṭāvakra Gītā, another great Advaitic text, offers parallel declarations:

> *Aham akhaṇḍa-bodho'ham* (1.15) — "I am undivided awareness."
> *Nirañjanaḥ nirākāro vibhur vyāpya sthitaḥ sadā* (1.18) — "Stainless, formless, all-pervading, I remain always."

These could serve as prose commentaries on Verse VI of the Nirvana Shatakam.

## Practical Application for Meditation

1. **Release all mental categories** — Sit quietly and notice how the mind constantly labels, sorts, and narrates. Instead of engaging with these vikalpas, turn attention to the awareness that *contains* them. That awareness is nirvikalpa.
   Affirm: *Aham nirvikalpaḥ* — "I am beyond all thought-constructs."

2. **Sense the formless** — Close your eyes. Feel the body, then ask: "Am I the shape of this body, or the awareness that knows the shape?" The awareness has no edges, no boundaries, no form.
   Affirm: *Nirākāra rūpaḥ* — "My nature is formless."

3. **Expand beyond the body** — Notice that awareness does not stop at the skin. The sounds you hear, the space around you — all arise within the same field of awareness. That awareness is *vibhu* — all-pervading.
   Affirm: *Vibhutvāchcha sarvatra* — "I pervade everything."

4. **Release even the spiritual search** — The desire for enlightenment is the last chain. Recognize: "There is no bondage from which I need to be freed. The Self is already free."
   Affirm: *Na chāsaṅgatam naiva muktiḥ* — "I am beyond bondage and liberation."

5. **Rest as the immeasurable**: *Chidānanda rūpaḥ Śhivo'ham Śhivo'ham.*

## Summary

Verse VI is the crown of the Nirvana Shatakam — the moment the Self declares itself positively after five verses of negation. The Self is **nirvikalpa** (beyond all thought), **nirākāra** (formless), **vibhu** (all-pervading through every sense, everywhere), **beyond both attachment and liberation**, and **immeasurable** — not an object that can be known, quantified, or grasped. It is the Subject behind all subjects, the Awareness within all awareness.

The hymn that began with the question *"Who are you?"* concludes with the answer that needs no further explanation: **Chidānanda rūpaḥ Śhivo'ham Śhivo'ham** — "I am of the nature of Consciousness-Bliss. I am Shiva. I am Shiva."

This is the answer to the ultimate question. This is *nirvāṇa* — not extinction, but the blazing forth of the Self's infinite, unconditioned, ever-blissful nature.

## Epilogue: The Hymn as a Whole

The six verses of the Nirvana Shatakam trace a complete spiritual journey:

| Verse | What Is Stripped | Layer of Identity |
|---|---|---|
| I | Mind, senses, elements | The thinking, perceiving, physical self |
| II | Prāṇa, body tissues, sheaths, organs of action | The living, breathing, acting self |
| III | Emotions, desires, the four goals of life | The feeling, wanting, aspiring self |
| IV | Karma, religion, the experiencer | The doing, practicing, experiencing self |
| V | Death, birth, caste, relationships | The mortal, social, relational self |
| VI | All limitation — culminating affirmation | The Self as it truly is: infinite, formless, free |

When everything is stripped away, what remains is not nothing — it is **everything**. Not the everything of the phenomenal world, but the everything of pure, undivided, self-luminous Consciousness-Bliss.

**This is the answer to "Who am I?"**
