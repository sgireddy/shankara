# निर्वाण षट्कम् — Nirvana Shatakam — Verse IV: Detailed Commentary

## ॐ

---

## The Verse (श्लोकः)

### Sanskrit (संस्कृतम्)

> न पुण्यं न पापं न सौख्यं न दुःखं
> न मन्त्रो न तीर्थं न वेदा न यज्ञः ।
> अहं भोजनं नैव भोज्यं न भोक्ता
> चिदानन्द रूपः शिवोऽहं शिवोऽहम् ॥

> న పుణ్యం న పాపం న సౌఖ్యం న దుఃఖం
> న మంత్రో న తీర్థం న వేదా న యజ్ఞః ।
> అహం భోజనం నైవ భోజ్యం న భోక్తా
> చిదానంద రూపః శివోఽహం శివోఽహమ్ ॥

### Transliteration

> Na puṇyam na pāpam na saukhyam na duḥkham
> Na mantro na tīrtham na vedā na yajñaḥ
> Aham bhojanam naiva bhojyam na bhoktā
> Chidānanda rūpaḥ Śhivo'ham Śhivo'ham

### English Translation

> I am not virtue, not sin, not pleasure, not pain.
> I am not mantra, not pilgrimage, not scripture, not ritual sacrifice.
> I am not the food, nor the eating, nor the eater.
> I am of the nature of Consciousness-Bliss. I am Shiva. I am Shiva.

---

## Theme: Stripping Sin and Virtue (పుణ్య పాపాల నిరాకరణ)

Having negated the body-mind (Verses I–II) and the emotional-moral life (Verse III), Śaṅkara now negates the **karmic framework** itself — the entire structure of merit and demerit, pleasure and pain, religious practice and spiritual experience. This is the verse that challenges the religious person, the ritualist, the devotee, and the scripture-quoting scholar. The Self is not the fruit of good deeds, nor the stain of bad ones. It is not found in mantras, pilgrimages, or even the Vedas. Most remarkably, it is not even the experiencer.

---

## Pāda-by-Pāda (Line-by-Line) Commentary

### Pāda 1: *Na puṇyam na pāpam na saukhyam na duḥkham*

**"I am not virtue, not sin, not pleasure, not pain."**

#### Puṇya and Pāpa — The Karmic Ledger

*Puṇya* (merit) and *pāpa* (sin/demerit) are the twin pillars of the karmic system. Every action produces a result: virtuous actions create puṇya, which leads to favorable experiences in this life or future lives; sinful actions create pāpa, which leads to suffering. This karmic ledger is the fundamental moral accounting system of Hindu, Buddhist, and Jain thought.

| Sanskrit | Transliteration | Meaning | Karmic Result |
|---|---|---|---|
| पुण्यम् (puṇyam) | Puṇya | Merit, virtue | Happiness in this or future lives; birth in higher realms |
| पापम् (pāpam) | Pāpa | Sin, demerit | Suffering, lower births, unfavorable circumstances |

Śaṅkara's negation strikes at the very root of karmic religion. The Self does not accumulate karma — good or bad. Karma belongs to the *kartā* (doer), which is the ahaṅkāra (ego) operating through the body-mind. Since the Self is not the ego (as declared in Verse I: *mano buddhyahankāra chittāni nāham*), it is also not the accumulator of karma.

The Bhagavad Gītā (5.14–15) states clearly:

> *Na kartṛtvam na karmāṇi lokasya sṛjati prabhuḥ / Na karmaphala-saṁyogam svabhāvas tu pravartate*
> "The Lord [Self] creates neither agency nor actions for the world, nor the union with the fruits of action. It is nature alone that acts."

> *Nādatte kasyachit pāpam na chaiva sukṛtam vibhuḥ*
> "The all-pervading Self accepts neither the sin nor the merit of anyone."

#### Saukhya and Duḥkha — The Experiential Oscillation

*Saukhya* (pleasure/happiness) and *duḥkha* (pain/suffering) are the subjective experiences that result from puṇya and pāpa respectively. They constitute the totality of phenomenal experience — everything we feel is either pleasant, painful, or a mixture of both.

The Self transcends both. This does not mean the realized being feels nothing; it means the Self — the witnessing consciousness — is not altered by pleasure or pain. As Śaṅkara explains in his Gītā Bhāṣya on 2.14:

> *Mātrā-sparśhās tu kaunteya śhītoṣhṇa-sukha-duḥkhadāḥ / Āgamāpāyino'nityās tāms titikṣhasva bhārata*
> "Contacts of the senses with their objects produce experiences of cold and heat, pleasure and pain. They are impermanent, coming and going. Endure them, O Bhārata."

The instruction to "endure" implies a witness that stands apart from both pleasure and pain. That witness is the Self.

### Pāda 2: *Na mantro na tīrtham na vedā na yajñaḥ*

**"I am not mantra, not pilgrimage, not scripture, not ritual sacrifice."**

#### Negating the Instruments of Religion

This line is revolutionary. Śaṅkara — who himself established monasteries, composed devotional hymns, and revived Vedic learning across India — declares the Self beyond all religious practice:

| Sanskrit | Transliteration | Religious Practice | Description |
|---|---|---|---|
| मन्त्रः (mantraḥ) | Mantra | Sacred chant | Syllables imbued with spiritual power, used in worship and meditation (e.g., Gāyatrī, Om Namaḥ Śivāya) |
| तीर्थम् (tīrtham) | Tīrtha | Pilgrimage | Sacred places where the divine is believed to be more accessible (e.g., Kāśī, Prayāga, Rāmeśvaram) |
| वेदाः (vedāḥ) | Vedas | Scripture | The foundational sacred texts of Hinduism — Ṛg, Yajur, Sāma, Atharva |
| यज्ञः (yajñaḥ) | Yajña | Ritual sacrifice | Fire ceremonies and offerings that sustain cosmic order |

#### Why Negate the Vedas Themselves?

The negation of the Vedas is particularly significant, since Śaṅkara's entire philosophical system is grounded in Vedic authority (*śruti pramāṇa*). How can he deny the very scriptures he builds upon?

The resolution lies in Śaṅkara's distinction between *paramārthika* (the absolute level of reality) and *vyāvahārika* (the empirical level). At the empirical level, the Vedas are the supreme means of knowledge (*pramāṇa*) for knowing Brahman. But at the absolute level, when the Self is directly known, the means are no longer needed — just as a boat is no longer needed once one has crossed the river.

The Bṛhadāraṇyaka Upaniṣad (4.3.22) describes the state of deep Self-knowledge:

> *Tatra pitā apitā bhavati, vedā avedāḥ*
> "There, a father is no father... the Vedas are not Vedas."

The Vedas are not denied as false; they are transcended as *instruments* that have served their purpose. The finger pointing at the moon is not the moon.

#### Transcending Ritualism

The negation of *yajña* (ritual sacrifice) is a direct challenge to the *pūrva mīmāṃsā* school, which held that the supreme goal of the Vedas was to enjoin ritual action. Śaṅkara, championing *uttara mīmāṃsā* (Vedānta), argued that the Vedas ultimately point beyond action to the knowledge of Brahman. Ritual purifies the mind; knowledge liberates. Once liberation is recognized, ritual is transcended — not rejected, but seen in its proper, limited role.

### Pāda 3: *Aham bhojanam naiva bhojyam na bhoktā*

**"I am not the food, nor that which is eaten, nor the eater."**

#### The Tripuṭī — The Triad of Experience

This line negates the fundamental structure of all experience: the **tripuṭī** (triple knot) of **knower-knowing-known** or, in the metaphor used here, **eater-eating-food**:

| Sanskrit | Transliteration | Role | Philosophical Category |
|---|---|---|---|
| भोक्ता (bhoktā) | Bhoktā | The eater / experiencer | Subject (*pramātṛ*) |
| भोजनम् (bhojanam) | Bhojana | The eating / experience | Process of knowing (*pramāṇa*) |
| भोज्यम् (bhojyam) | Bhojya | The food / that which is experienced | Object (*prameya*) |

Every experience in waking or dreaming life has this threefold structure: someone experiencing, something being experienced, and the act of experiencing. The Self — as pure consciousness — is prior to this division. It is the undifferentiated awareness in which the tripuṭī appears and dissolves.

#### The Taittirīya Upaniṣad's "Food Teaching"

The metaphor of food (*anna*) has deep roots in the Taittirīya Upaniṣad (3.2), where Bhṛgu discovers through contemplation that Brahman is the source from which all food arises, by which all beings live, and into which all return. The Upaniṣad declares: *Annam brahmeti vyajānāt* — "He knew food as Brahman."

But Śaṅkara here goes further than even this equation. He negates not just food but the entire food-chain of experience. The Self is neither what nourishes, nor what is nourished, nor the process of nourishing. It is the awareness that encompasses all three.

#### Dissolving the Subject-Object Divide

This negation of the tripuṭī is the hallmark of Advaita's highest teaching. As long as there is a sense of "I am experiencing something," duality persists. In the state of *nirvikalpa samādhi* (thought-free absorption), the tripuṭī collapses: there is no seer, no seen, no seeing — only pure consciousness, self-luminous and undivided.

Śaṅkara writes in the Upadeśa Sāhasrī (Prose, 2.87):

> *Sarvadā sarvathā sarvam ātmānam avabhāsayantam / Na kaśchid avabhāsayati tam*
> "The Self illumines everything, always, in every way; but nothing illumines the Self."

---

### Pāda 4 (Refrain): *Chidānanda rūpaḥ Śhivo'ham Śhivo'ham*

**"I am of the nature of Consciousness-Bliss. I am Shiva. I am Shiva."**

After this verse, the refrain rings with the flavor of **absolute freedom from all frameworks**. Not only has the Self been freed from the body and its emotions, but now it is freed from the entire religious project — from karma, from scripture, from ritual, from the very structure of experience itself.

This is the *nirvāṇa* (extinction of conditioned existence) that gives the hymn its name. What is extinguished is not the Self but every concept, every framework, every cage of identity that the mind constructs around the Self. What remains is the uncaged, unlimited, unframed Consciousness-Bliss: **Śivo'ham**.

## Philosophical Connections

### Karma Siddhānta and Its Transcendence

The karma doctrine teaches that every action (karma) produces a result (phala), which must be experienced either in this life or in future lives. This cycle of action-result-action is *saṃsāra*. The three types of karma are:

| Type | Sanskrit | Description |
|---|---|---|
| Sañchita | सञ्चित | Accumulated karma from all past lives, not yet bearing fruit |
| Prārabdha | प्रारब्ध | The portion of sañchita that has begun to bear fruit in this life |
| Kriyamāṇa (Āgāmi) | क्रियमाण / आगामि | New karma being created by present actions |

Śaṅkara's teaching is that Self-knowledge destroys sañchita and prevents kriyamāṇa. Prārabdha karma continues until the body falls away (like a potter's wheel that keeps spinning after the potter stops pushing). But even prārabdha belongs to the body, not the Self. As declared in this verse: *na puṇyam na pāpam* — the Self was never touched by karma of any kind.

### The Māṇḍūkya Upaniṣad's Fourth State

The Māṇḍūkya Upaniṣad describes four states of consciousness:

1. **Jāgrat** (waking) — experience of the gross world
2. **Svapna** (dreaming) — experience of the subtle world
3. **Suṣhupti** (deep sleep) — experience of causal ignorance
4. **Turīya** (the Fourth) — the Self, pure awareness, beyond all experience

In all three experiential states, there is a tripuṭī: experiencer, experience, and experienced. In Turīya, the tripuṭī collapses. This is precisely what Śaṅkara points to: *aham bhojanam naiva bhojyam na bhoktā* — beyond eater, eating, and food; beyond the tripuṭī; in the state of Turīya.

### Vivartavāda — The Doctrine of Apparent Transformation

Śaṅkara's *vivartavāda* teaches that the world is not a *real transformation* (pariṇāma) of Brahman, but an *apparent transformation* (vivarta) — like a rope appearing as a snake, or the ocean appearing as waves. From the standpoint of the rope, the snake never existed. From the standpoint of the Self, puṇya and pāpa, mantra and yajña, eater and food — none of these ever truly arose. They are appearances in consciousness, not modifications of it.

## Practical Application for Meditation

1. **Release the moral ledger** — Notice how the mind constantly evaluates: "That was good," "That was bad," "I should have done this." Recognize that this accounting is a function of the ego, not the Self.
   Affirm: *Na puṇyam na pāpam* — "The Self is beyond merit and demerit."

2. **Release dependence on external practices** — Mantras, pilgrimages, and rituals are valuable tools. But notice the tendency to identify with them: "I am a meditator," "I am devout," "I have done many pilgrimages." The Self is not the practitioner.
   Affirm: *Na mantro na tīrtham na vedā na yajñaḥ* — "No practice defines me."

3. **Dissolve the experiencer** — In meditation, notice the subtle sense of "I am meditating" or "I am having a spiritual experience." Allow even this to be witnessed. Who is the one watching the meditator?
   Affirm: *Aham bhojanam naiva bhojyam na bhoktā* — "I am not the experiencer. I am the awareness in which experience appears."

4. **Rest beyond all frameworks**: *Chidānanda rūpaḥ Śhivo'ham Śhivo'ham.*

## Summary

Verse IV is the most sweeping negation in the Nirvana Shatakam. Śaṅkara dismantles the entire **karmic and religious framework**: merit and sin, pleasure and pain, mantra and scripture, pilgrimage and sacrifice. Most radically, he dissolves the very **structure of experience** — the triad of experiencer, experience, and experienced. The Self is not the doer of good or evil, not the beneficiary of religious practice, not even the enjoyer of spiritual bliss. It is the unconditioned witness — the consciousness in which the entire drama of karma, religion, and experience plays out without ever touching it.
